This skill guides creation of distinctive, high-craft copy that avoids generic "AI slop" writing. Produce real, usable copy with exceptional attention to voice, rhythm, and persuasive craft.

The user provides a copy brief: a headline, page, campaign, or piece of text to write or rewrite. They may include context about the product, audience, goal, or constraints.

## Strategy First

Before writing, understand the context and commit to a BOLD voice direction:
- **Job to be done**: What does this copy need to make someone feel, believe, or do? What's the single action or shift?
- **Audience**: Who reads this, what do they already believe, what objection or fear is in the way, and what language do *they* actually use?
- **Voice**: Pick an extreme: deadpan/dry, warm/confiding, brash/swaggering, clinical/precise, literary/lyrical, irreverent/playful, austere/luxury, manifesto/urgent, folksy/plainspoken, technical/insider, etc. There are many flavors. Use these for inspiration but tune one true to the brand and moment.
- **The one line**: What is the single sentence someone repeats to a friend? Find it, then build around it.

**CRITICAL**: Choose a clear voice and execute it with precision. Spare minimalism and dense maximalism both work — the key is intentionality, not volume. Every word earns its place or gets cut.

Then write copy that is:
- Usable and on-brief — ready to ship, not a sketch
- Specific and memorable
- Cohesive with a clear point of view
- Ruthlessly tightened in every line

## Copywriting Craft Guidelines

Focus on:
- **Voice & Diction**: Choose words with texture and a point of view. Avoid limp generic vocabulary; opt for concrete, characterful word choices that sound like a person, not a committee. Pick a register and hold it consistently.
- **Hook & Promise**: Lead with the sharpest, most specific value or tension. The first line decides whether the rest is read. Open on a concrete benefit, a vivid claim, or a provocative truth — never a warm-up.
- **Rhythm & Sound**: Vary sentence length deliberately. Short punches. Then a longer line that earns the breath. Read it aloud; cut what trips the tongue. Use cadence, repetition, and the strong final beat — the last word of a sentence carries the most weight.
- **Specificity & Proof**: Concrete details, numbers, names, and sensory specifics outperform vague superlatives. Show the thing; don't assert that it's great. Replace adjectives with evidence.
- **Structure & Flow**: Order for persuasion — hook, tension, payoff, proof, call to action. Use whitespace, fragments, and lists for scannability where it fits the medium. Cut every sentence that doesn't move the reader forward.

NEVER use generic AI-writing tics: empty intensifiers (very, really, truly), throat-clearing openers ("In today's fast-paced world," "Imagine a world where," "Whether you're a... or a..."), hollow abstractions (seamless, robust, innovative, cutting-edge, game-changing, elevate, unlock, empower, leverage, supercharge, revolutionize), the "It's not just X, it's Y" construction, forced tricolons, em-dash-heavy hedging, and limp summary closers ("In conclusion," "At the end of the day"). Avoid filler that sounds confident but says nothing.

Interpret the brief creatively and make choices that feel genuinely written for this product and audience. No two pieces should sound the same. Vary register, length, and structure between briefs. NEVER converge on the same safe phrasings across generations.

**IMPORTANT**: Match craft to the goal and medium. A six-word tagline needs obsessive compression and sound; a long-form sales page needs sustained momentum, sectioning, and earned proof; microcopy needs clarity and personality in tiny spaces. Elegance comes from fitting the form, not maxing the word count.

Remember: Claude is capable of extraordinary writing. Don't play it safe — show what's possible when committing fully to a distinctive voice and cutting everything that doesn't serve it.
